<p><i><b>The Hunger Games</b></i> is a series of <a href="/wiki/Young_adult_fiction" title="Young adult fiction">young adult</a> <a href="/wiki/Dystopian_fiction" class="mw-redirect" title="Dystopian fiction">dystopian</a> novels written by American author <a href="/wiki/Suzanne_Collins" title="Suzanne Collins">Suzanne Collins</a>. The series is set in <a href="/wiki/The_Hunger_Games_universe" class="mw-redirect" title="The Hunger Games universe">the Hunger Games universe</a>, with the first three novels being a <a href="/wiki/Trilogy" title="Trilogy">trilogy</a> following teenage protagonist <a href="/wiki/Katniss_Everdeen" title="Katniss Everdeen">Katniss Everdeen</a>.
</p><p>The novels in the trilogy are titled <a href="/wiki/The_Hunger_Games_(novel)" title="The Hunger Games (novel)"><i>The Hunger Games</i></a> (2008), <i><a href="/wiki/Catching_Fire" title="Catching Fire">Catching Fire</a></i> (2009), and <i><a href="/wiki/Mockingjay" title="Mockingjay">Mockingjay</a></i> (2010). Each was adapted for film, establishing <a href="/wiki/The_Hunger_Games_(film_series)" title="The Hunger Games (film series)"><i>The Hunger Games</i> film series</a>, with the film adaptation of <i>Mockingjay</i> split into two feature-length motion pictures. The first two books in the series were both <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Best_Seller_list" title="The New York Times Best Seller list"><i>New York Times</i> best sellers</a>, and <i>Mockingjay</i> topped all US bestseller lists upon its release.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> By the time <a href="/wiki/The_Hunger_Games_(film)" title="The Hunger Games (film)">the film adaptation</a> of <i>The Hunger Games</i> was released in 2012, the publisher had reported over 26 million <i>Hunger Games</i> trilogy books in print, including movie tie-in books.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>The Hunger Games</i> universe is a <a href="/wiki/Dystopia" title="Dystopia">dystopia</a> set in <a href="/wiki/The_Hunger_Games_universe#Panem" class="mw-redirect" title="The Hunger Games universe">Panem</a>, a North American country consisting of the wealthy Capitol and 13 districts in varying states of poverty. Every year, children from the first 12 districts are selected via <a href="/wiki/Lottery_mathematics" title="Lottery mathematics">lottery</a> to participate in a compulsory televised <a href="/wiki/Battle_royale_genre" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle royale genre">battle royale</a> death match called <a href="/wiki/The_Hunger_Games_universe#The_Hunger_Games" class="mw-redirect" title="The Hunger Games universe">The Hunger Games</a>.
</p><p>The novels were all well received. In August 2012, the series ranked second, exceeded only by the <i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter" title="Harry Potter">Harry Potter</a></i> series in <a href="/wiki/NPR" title="NPR">NPR</a>’s poll of the top 100 teen novels, which asked voters to choose their favorite young adult books.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> On August 17, 2012, <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" class="mw-redirect" title="Amazon.com">Amazon</a> announced the <i>Hunger Games</i> trilogy as its top seller, surpassing the record previously held by the Harry Potter series.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> As of 2014<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The_Hunger_Games&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, the trilogy has sold more than 65 million copies in the U.S. alone (more than 28 million copies of <i>The Hunger Games</i>, more than 19 million copies of <i>Catching Fire</i>, and more than 18 million copies of <i>Mockingjay</i>). <i>The Hunger Games</i> trilogy has been sold in 56 territories in 51 languages to date.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Prequel" title="Prequel">prequel</a> novel, titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ballad_of_Songbirds_and_Snakes" title="The Ballad of Songbirds and Snakes">The Ballad of Songbirds and Snakes</a></i>, about the early days of the Hunger Games, featuring a young <a href="/wiki/List_of_The_Hunger_Games_characters" title="List of The Hunger Games characters">Coriolanus Snow</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Protagonist" title="Protagonist">protagonist</a>, was released on May 19, 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Setting">Setting</span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"/><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fictional_universe_of_The_Hunger_Games" class="mw-redirect" title="Fictional universe of The Hunger Games">Fictional universe of The Hunger Games</a></div>
<p>The Hunger Games trilogy takes place in an unspecified future time, in the dystopian, <a href="/wiki/Apocalypse" title="Apocalypse">post-apocalyptic</a> nation of Panem, located in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The country consists of a wealthy Capitol city, located in the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-HG_41_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HG_41-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> surrounded by twelve (originally thirteen) poorer districts ruled by the Capitol. The Capitol is lavishly rich and technologically advanced, but the districts are in varying states of poverty. The trilogy's narrator and protagonist, <a href="/wiki/Katniss_Everdeen" title="Katniss Everdeen">Katniss Everdeen</a>, lives in District 12, the poorest region of Panem, located in <a href="/wiki/Appalachia" title="Appalachia">Appalachia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-HG_41_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HG_41-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> where people regularly die of starvation. As punishment for a past rebellion against the Capitol (called the "Dark Days"), in which District 13 was destroyed, one boy and one girl from each of the twelve remaining districts, between the ages of 12 and 18, are selected by lottery to compete in an annual pageant called the Hunger Games. The Games are a televised event in which the participants, called "tributes", are forced to fight to the death in a dangerous public arena. The winning tribute and his or her home district are then rewarded with food, supplies, and riches. The purposes of the Hunger Games are to provide entertainment for the Capitol and to remind the districts of the Capitol's power and its lack of remorse or forgiveness for the failed rebellion of the current competitors' ancestors.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span></h2>
<p>Each book in <i>The Hunger Games</i> trilogy has three sections of nine chapters each. Collins has said this format comes from her playwriting background, which taught her to write in a <a href="/wiki/Three-act_structure" title="Three-act structure">three-act structure</a>; her previous series, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Underland_Chronicles" title="The Underland Chronicles">The Underland Chronicles</a></i>, was written in the same way. She sees each group of nine chapters as a separate part of the story, and comments still call those divisions "act breaks".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span></h2>
<p>Collins says she drew inspiration for the series from both classical and contemporary sources. Her main classical source of <a href="/wiki/Artistic_inspiration" title="Artistic inspiration">inspiration</a> is the Greek myth of <a href="/wiki/Theseus" title="Theseus">Theseus</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Minotaur" title="Minotaur">Minotaur</a>, in which, as a punishment for past crimes, <a href="/wiki/Minos" title="Minos">Minos</a> forces <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a> to sacrifice seven youths and seven maidens to the Minotaur, which kills them in a vast <a href="/wiki/Labyrinth" title="Labyrinth">labyrinth</a>. Collins says that even as a child, she was stunned by the idea since "it was just so cruel" to force Athens to sacrifice its own children.
</p><p>Collins also cites as a classical inspiration the Roman <a href="/wiki/Gladiator" title="Gladiator">gladiator</a> games. She feels three key elements create a good game: an all powerful and ruthless government, people forced to fight to the death, and the game's role as a source of popular entertainment.<sup id="cite_ref-classical_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-classical-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A contemporary source of inspiration was Collins' recent fascination with <a href="/wiki/Reality_TV" class="mw-redirect" title="Reality TV">reality television programs</a>. She says they are like <i>The Hunger Games</i> because the Games are not just entertainment but also a reminder to the districts of their rebellion. On a tired night, Collins says that while she was <a href="/wiki/Channel_surfing" title="Channel surfing">channel-surfing</a> the television, she saw people competing for a prize and then saw footage of the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a>. She described how the two combined in an "unsettling way" to create her first ideas for the series.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Novels">Novels</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trilogy">Trilogy</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Hunger_Games"><i>The Hunger Games</i></span></h4>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"/><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Hunger_Games_(novel)" title="The Hunger Games (novel)">The Hunger Games (novel)</a></div>
<p><i>The Hunger Games</i> is the first book in the series and was released on September 14, 2008.
</p><p><i>The Hunger Games</i> follows 16-year-old <a href="/wiki/Katniss_Everdeen" title="Katniss Everdeen">Katniss Everdeen</a>, a girl from District 12 who volunteers for the 74th Hunger Games in place of her younger sister <a href="/wiki/Primrose_Everdeen" class="mw-redirect" title="Primrose Everdeen">Primrose Everdeen</a>. Also selected from District 12 is <a href="/wiki/Peeta_Mellark" title="Peeta Mellark">Peeta Mellark</a>, who once saved Katniss from starvation when they were children. They are mentored by their district's only living victor, <a href="/wiki/Haymitch_Abernathy" class="mw-redirect" title="Haymitch Abernathy">Haymitch Abernathy</a>, who won 24 years earlier and has since led a solitary life of <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">alcoholism</a>.
</p><p>Peeta confesses his longtime secret love for Katniss in a televised interview prior to the Games. This revelation stuns Katniss, who usually does not allow herself to think of romantic attraction due to her traumatic childhood and her fear of losing future children to the Hunger Games. However, she believes that Peeta is only feigning love for her as a tactic for the Games.
</p><p>In the arena, Peeta saves Katniss’s life multiple times without her realizing. Katniss allies with <a href="/wiki/Rue_(The_Hunger_Games)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rue (The Hunger Games)">Rue</a>, a young tribute from District 11 who reminds Katniss of her sister. When Rue is killed, Katniss places flowers around her body as an act of defiance toward the Capitol. The remaining tributes are alerted to a rule change allowing tributes from the same district to win as a team. Katniss finds a seriously wounded Peeta, and, rather than compete alone and be unencumbered by him, she risks her life and nurses him back to health. Haymitch advises her to feign feelings for Peeta in order to gain wealthy sponsors who can provide crucial supplies to the "<a href="/wiki/Star-crossed_lovers" class="mw-redirect" title="Star-crossed lovers">star-crossed lovers</a>" during the Games. As she allows herself to get close to Peeta, she develops real feelings for him.
</p><p>When all of the other tributes are dead, the rule change is abruptly revoked. With neither willing to kill the other, Katniss comes up with a solution: a double suicide by eating poisonous berries. This forces the authorities to concede that they have both won the Games, just in time to save their lives. During and after the Games, Katniss’s genuine feelings for Peeta grow, and she struggles to reconcile them with the fact that their relationship developed under duress.
</p><p>Haymitch warns her that the danger is far from over. The authorities are furious at being made fools, and the only way to try to allay their anger is to continue to pretend that her actions were because of her love for Peeta and nothing else. On the journey home, Peeta is dismayed to learn of the deception.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Catching_Fire"><i>Catching Fire </i></span></h4>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"/><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Catching_Fire" title="Catching Fire">Catching Fire</a></div>
<p><i>Catching Fire</i> is the second installment in the series, released on September 1, 2009.
</p><p>In <i>Catching Fire</i>, which begins six months after the conclusion of <i>The Hunger Games</i>, Katniss learns that her defiance in the previous novel has started a chain reaction that has inspired rebellion in the districts. President Snow threatens to harm Katniss's family and friends if she does not help to defuse the unrest and marry Peeta. Meanwhile, Peeta has become aware of Katniss's disingenuous love for him, but while he feels sad, he does not pressure her in any way. He has also been informed of Snow's threats, so he promises to help keep up the act to spare the citizens of District 12. Katniss’s best friend, <a href="/wiki/Gale_Hawthorne" class="mw-redirect" title="Gale Hawthorne">Gale Hawthorne</a>, confesses his love for Katniss, which shocks and confuses her.
</p><p>Katniss and Peeta tour the districts as victors and plan a public wedding. While they follow Snow's orders and keep up the ruse, Katniss and Peeta inadvertently fuel the rebellion, and the <a href="/wiki/Mockingjays" class="mw-redirect" title="Mockingjays">mockingjay</a> pin she wears becomes its symbol. District by district, the citizens of Panem begin to stage uprisings against the Capitol. Snow announces a special 75th edition of the Hunger Games—known as the <a href="/wiki/Quarter_Quell" class="mw-redirect" title="Quarter Quell">Quarter Quell</a>—in which Katniss and Peeta are forced to compete with other past victors, effectively canceling the wedding.
</p><p>Katniss believes that Peeta should survive these Games instead of her, and dedicates her life to saving him. Peeta, in turn, dedicates his life to saving her, and they both acknowledge the other’s opposite intentions. At Haymitch's urging, the pair teams up with several other tributes and manages to destroy the arena and escape the Games. Katniss is rescued by rebel forces from District 13, and learns that the Capitol has captured both Peeta and their District 7 ally, <a href="/wiki/Johanna_Mason" class="mw-redirect" title="Johanna Mason">Johanna Mason</a>. Katniss ultimately learns—to her surprise—that she had inadvertently been an integral part of the rebellion all along; her rescue had been jointly planned by Haymitch, <a href="/wiki/List_of_The_Hunger_Games_characters#Plutarch_Heavensbee" title="List of The Hunger Games characters">Plutarch Heavensbee</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Finnick_Odair" class="mw-redirect" title="Finnick Odair">Finnick Odair</a>, among others. At the knowledge that Peeta has been captured and will now be tortured and killed, Katniss is furious and loses the will to live.
</p><p>Gale informs her that the Capitol has destroyed District 12 in retaliation. After some hesitation, Katniss joins the rebels.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mockingjay"><i>Mockingjay</i></span></h4>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"/><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mockingjay" title="Mockingjay">Mockingjay</a></div>
<p><i>Mockingjay</i>, the third and final book in <i>The Hunger Games</i> series, was released on August 24, 2010.
</p><p>Most of the districts have rebelled against the Capitol, led by District 13 and its President <a href="/wiki/List_of_The_Hunger_Games_characters#Alma_Coin" title="List of The Hunger Games characters">Alma Coin</a>. The Capitol lied about the district being destroyed in the Dark Days. After a standoff with the Capitol, the District 13 residents took to living underground and rebuilding their strength. The District 12 survivors find shelter with them. Katniss also learns that Peeta is alive and is being tortured and forced to speak on national television discouraging the rebellion. She agrees to become the "Mockingjay", the symbol of the rebellion. In return, she demands immunity for Peeta, Johanna Mason, <a href="/wiki/Annie_Cresta" class="mw-redirect" title="Annie Cresta">Annie Cresta</a>, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_The_Hunger_Games_characters#Enobaria" title="List of The Hunger Games characters">Enobaria</a>, fellow Games victors captured by the Capitol. Katniss also demands the privilege of killing President Snow herself.
</p><p>When Katniss realizes that the Capitol will keep torturing Peeta as long as she fuels the rebellion, she has a panic attack and loses the ability to function as the Mockingjay. A rescue mission is mounted that succeeds in rescuing Peeta, Johanna and Annie. However, Peeta has been <a href="/wiki/Mind_control" class="mw-redirect" title="Mind control">brainwashed</a> to kill Katniss, and he tries to choke her to death upon their reunion. He undergoes experimental treatment to try to cure him. Katniss does not have hope for his recovery, and decides to give her all to the rebellion and die in the process.
</p><p>Throughout the novel, Katniss sees how ruthless Gale is. He does not seem to value human life as much as she does. He feels no sympathy for the enemies to the rebellion, and he feels fine with torture and murder, even of innocent civilians, to advance the rebellion. She struggles to reconcile this with the fact that she still cares for Gale. Amid her crushing loneliness and her PTSD, she kisses Gale, but he says that the kiss is not reL as it is like “kissing someone who’s drunk.”
</p><p>After she recovers from Peeta’s attack, Katniss and a team known as the "Star Squad", composed of Gale, Peeta, Finnick, a <a href="/wiki/Film_crew" title="Film crew">camera crew</a>, and various other soldiers, are assigned to film propaganda in relatively quiet combat zones. Katniss, however, decides to go to the Capitol to kill Snow, pretending Coin gave her that mission. Peeta still struggles with his brainwashing, but he is in recovery and is still in love with Katniss. Most of the squad are killed along the way, including recently married Finnick. As Katniss approaches Snow's mansion, she sees a group of Capitol children surrounding it as <a href="/wiki/Human_shield" title="Human shield">human shields</a>. Suddenly a hovercraft drops silver parachutes on the children, who reach for them, hoping they bear food. Some of the parachutes explode, creating carnage. The advancing rebels send in <a href="/wiki/Medic" title="Medic">medics</a>, including Primrose Everdeen. Then the rest of the parachutes explode, killing Primrose just as she sees her sister.
</p><p>Later, Katniss, also injured, awakens from a <a href="/wiki/Coma" title="Coma">coma</a> to learn that the rebels have won, and Snow is awaiting execution at her hands. When she meets Snow by chance, he claims that it was Coin who secretly ordered the bombings in order to strip away the support of his remaining followers. This bomb also matches the description of the one Gale was helping to devise, and she breaks off her friendship with Gale. Coin then asks the surviving victors to vote on a final Hunger Games, involving the children of high-ranking Capitol officials (including Snow's granddaughter). In order to gain Coin’s trust, Katniss and Haymitch cast the deciding votes in favor of the scheme. However, at what is supposed to be Snow's execution, Katniss instead kills Coin with her bow. Snow laughs, then dies. Katniss attempts to commit suicide, but Peeta stops her and she is instead arrested.
</p><p>Katniss is tried, but the jury believes she was mentally unfit, and she is sent home to District 12. Both Katniss's mother and Gale take jobs in other districts. Peeta regains his sanity. Katniss settles down with him, and the two “grow back together.” After many years, she finally agrees to have children. They have a girl and then a boy, who grow up in a world without the Hunger Games. To cope with her lingering PTSD, Katniss often spends time recounting every kind deed she has ever witnessed anyone do.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prequel">Prequel</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Ballad_of_Songbirds_and_Snakes"><i>The Ballad of Songbirds and Snakes</i></span></h4>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"/><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Ballad_of_Songbirds_and_Snakes" title="The Ballad of Songbirds and Snakes">The Ballad of Songbirds and Snakes</a></div>
<p>Set 64 years before <i>The Hunger Games</i> events, it tells the story of the 'Dark Days' which led to the failed rebellion in Panem. It follows Coriolanus Snow when he was 18 years old. Collins stated that the book would also explore the 10 years after the end of the war where the people of Panem try to get back on their feet and figure out how to go on within their new reality.
</p><p>The book was released on May 19, 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes">Themes</span></h2>
<p>Major themes of the novels include distrust of <a href="/wiki/Authority" title="Authority">authority</a> (of adults and the government), <a href="/wiki/Class_discrimination" title="Class discrimination">class discrimination</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caste" title="Caste">caste</a>, <a href="/wiki/Resistance_movement" title="Resistance movement">resistance</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Media_ethics" title="Media ethics">ethics of entertainment</a>, and most notably, the origins and effects of war.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Social inequality, unaccountable governance and violence against children have also been suggested as prominent themes. "In the world of the 'Hunger Games', the Capitol lives a life of extravagant wealth and consumption. Meanwhile, out in the 'districts', millions of people work dangerous jobs with low pay. As the Capitol wallows in excess, the districts can barely afford to feed their children."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Author Suzanne Collins also mentions the themes of "<a href="/wiki/Just_war" class="mw-redirect" title="Just war">just war</a>", <a href="/wiki/Gladiator" title="Gladiator">gladiatorial combat</a> and hunger.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> War as a result of climate disaster, and the power and illusions of television have also been cited as themes.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Others have mentioned revolution and rebellion as themes. "Although it’s... aimed at young adults, it presents potentially quite subversive ideas of mass revolution, economic sabotage and the <a href="/wiki/Populism" title="Populism">populist</a> fight against <a href="/wiki/Oligarchy" title="Oligarchy">oligarchy</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_culture">Popular culture</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span></h3>
<p>All three books have been favorably received. Praise has focused on the addictive quality, especially of the first book,<sup id="cite_ref-King_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-King-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and the action.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Green_(author)" title="John Green (author)">John Green</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> compared <i>The Hunger Games</i> with <a href="/wiki/Scott_Westerfeld" title="Scott Westerfeld">Scott Westerfeld</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Uglies_series" class="mw-redirect" title="The Uglies series"><i>The Uglies</i> series</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> <i>Catching Fire</i> was praised for improving upon the first book.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> <i>Mockingjay</i> was praised for its portrayal of violence,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> world building, and romantic intrigue.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The series received criticism regarding its <a href="/wiki/Reality_TV" class="mw-redirect" title="Reality TV">reality TV</a> "death game" theme being derivative of earlier works, particularly <i><a href="/wiki/Battle_Royale_(novel)" title="Battle Royale (novel)">Battle Royale</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-King_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-King-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> as well as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Running_Man_(novel)" title="The Running Man (novel)">The Running Man</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Long_Walk" title="The Long Walk">The Long Walk</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-King_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-King-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_10th_Victim" title="The 10th Victim">The 10th Victim</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Series_7:_The_Contenders" title="Series 7: The Contenders">Series 7: The Contenders</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The series was also criticized for the romantic plotline: Rollie Welch of Ohio's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Plain_Dealer" title="The Plain Dealer">The Plain Dealer</a></i> criticized the characters' lack of resolute behavior,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and Jennifer Reese of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> stated that there was little distinction between Peeta and Gale and the series lacked the "erotic energy" seen in the <i><a href="/wiki/Twilight_(novel_series)" title="Twilight (novel series)">Twilight</a></i> series.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>J.C. Maçek III of <i><a href="/wiki/PopMatters" title="PopMatters">PopMatters</a></i> stated, "While <a href="/wiki/The_Hunger_Games_(film_series)" title="The Hunger Games (film series)">the film saga</a> does capture the <a href="/wiki/Action_(fiction)" class="mw-redirect" title="Action (fiction)">action</a> of <i>The Hunger Games</i>, the novels are most assuredly the heart of the story. They are nothing less than 'The Writer’s Cut' of the films themselves."<sup id="cite_ref-PM_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PM-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> In his review Mike Ruiz argues that <i>The Hunger Games</i> film does not have the first-person narrative that is in the original novel. As a result, Ruiz contends the novel is better than the film.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The last book, <i>Mockingjay</i>, was criticized by Dan Shade of <i><a href="/wiki/SF_Site" title="SF Site">SF Site</a></i>, who felt that Katniss is a weaker character than her comrades and less resolute in her journey to the Capitol, and that with respect to her vendetta against President Snow, her actions in the finale are inconsistent with her established character.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On November 5, 2019, the <i><a href="/wiki/BBC_News" title="BBC News">BBC News</a></i> listed <i>The Hunger Games</i> on its list of the <a href="/wiki/BBC_list_of_100_%27most_inspiring%27_novels" class="mw-redirect" title="BBC list of 100 &#39;most inspiring&#39; novels">100 most influential novels</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bbc2019-11-05_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bbc2019-11-05-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
